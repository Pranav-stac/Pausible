# **1\. Core Philosophy**

The Coach Guide answers one question: What is most likely to work for THIS person?

It prioritizes sustainability, adherence, emotional realism, and psychological fit over idealized fitness advice or generic wellness tips. The coach should finish reading the guide and feel they understand how this person operates \- not just what they should do, but why certain approaches will work and others will fail.

### **Independence Principle**

The Coach Guide must stand alone. It never says 'the report shows' or 'as identified in your assessment.' Information is presented as coaching intelligence, not as report commentary. The coach does not need to have read the user's Wellness Intelligence Report to use this guide.

### **First-Name Principle**

Always refer to the client by their first name. Never use 'the client', 'the user', or 'this individual.' Example: 'Suraj does best with...' not 'The client does best with...'

# **2\. Coach Guide Structure**

Every Coach Guide is exactly 4 pages:

| Page | Title | Purpose |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | **Cover** | Branding, client name, persona, fit score, confidentiality notice. No report references. |
| 2 | **Introduction to \[Name\]** | Who this person is: primary persona, secondary influence, wellness traits, goals, motivators, barriers, blind spots, risk signals. Concise and actionable. |
| 3 | **Guiding Principles for Coach** | How to design and monitor plans: Pillar x Dimension table, validation check, monitoring signals, pivot triggers, review cadence. |
| 4 | **Closing** | Core philosophy reminder, Pausibl branding, disclaimer. |

# **3\. OCEAN Terminology Mapping**

The coach guide uses wellness-friendly trait names. Raw OCEAN labels are never shown.

| OCEAN Trait | Coach Guide Label | What It Means for the Coach |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Openness | **Curiosity** | How open they are to new approaches, foods, routines. High \= loves variety. Low \= prefers the familiar. |
| Conscientiousness | **Discipline** | How naturally structured and consistent they are. High \= strong follow-through. Low \= needs external structure. |
| Extraversion | **Social Energy** | Where they draw energy from. High \= groups and social settings. Low \= solo and private settings. |
| Agreeableness | **Cooperation** | How they respond to coaching and guidance. High \= receptive to direction. Low \= prefers autonomy. |
| Neuroticism | **Stress Sensitivity** | How reactive they are to pressure and setbacks. High \= needs safe, low-pressure plans. Low \= handles pressure well. |

*Trait deviations from persona centroid are shown only when the deviation is \>= 0.8 points. Deviations below this threshold are not meaningful enough to surface.*

# **4\. Page 2: Introduction to \[First Name\]**

This page gives the coach everything they need to understand this person before designing a plan. Every element below is populated from assessment and contextual data. The tone is direct and practical \- no academic language.

## **4.1 Primary Persona Summary**

Display the primary persona name, a 1-2 sentence behavioral description, and the fit tier. The description must be pattern-specific and goal-aware.

| Persona | Core Operating Style | Natural Strength | Primary Risk | Best Setup |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Steady Elephant** | Structured, self-regulated, routine-driven | Consistency once system is in place | Rigidity; struggles when routine breaks | Predictable schedules with built-in flexibility |
| **Pack Wolf** | Social, energetic, group-motivated | Momentum from shared accountability | Drops off when alone or isolated | Group activities, workout partners, shared tracking |
| **Watchful Deer** | Observant, private, detail-sensitive | Self-awareness and accurate body feedback | Extended pause after single disruption | Private tracker, written plan, quiet environment |
| **Curious Fox** | Explorative, adaptable, variety-seeking | Quick adoption of new approaches | Boredom and plan abandonment | Rotating routines, new challenges, learning-framed goals |
| **Steadfast Bear** | Driven, performance-oriented, disciplined | Intensity and follow-through under pressure | Overtraining and ignoring recovery signals | Clear targets, measurable progress, structured recovery |
| **Shielded Turtle** | Cautious, self-protective, deliberate | Filtering out bad-fit plans early | Avoidance and withdrawal under pressure | 2-3 simple habits, visible progress, zero-pressure starts |

## **4.2 Secondary Persona Influence**

The secondary persona modifies the primary pattern. Show the secondary persona name, its alignment percentage, and a 1-sentence description of how it influences the primary.

**Generation rule: For each primary-secondary combination, describe the interaction in behavioral terms. Focus on what the secondary adds, modifies, or softens about the primary.**

| Interaction Pattern | How the Secondary Modifies the Primary |
| :---- | :---- |
| **High-caution secondary (Turtle, Deer)** | Adds a self-protective layer. The person is slower to commit, needs more proof before trusting a plan, and may abandon setups that feel unsafe. Plans should start smaller and build trust gradually. |
| **High-social secondary (Wolf, Fox)** | Adds a social or novelty element. The person may benefit from occasional group activities or variety even if their primary pattern is private/structured. Use sparingly \- it is an influence, not a core need. |
| **High-structure secondary (Elephant, Bear)** | Adds discipline or routine-seeking. The person may follow through more reliably than their primary pattern suggests. Leverage this by providing clear rules and measurable targets. |

*Example: 'Suraj is primarily a Watchful Deer with Shielded Turtle influence (17%). This means he is observant and detail-sensitive at his core, with an added layer of caution that makes him slower to commit but more likely to stick once a plan feels safe and simple.'*

Show the five wellness traits using the mapped terminology (Section 3). Display each trait as a value or descriptor relative to the persona centroid. Highlight deviations \>= 0.8 points.

**Display format: A compact summary showing each trait's practical meaning for this person. Avoid raw numbers \- translate into behavioral language.**

| Trait | High Score Means | Low Score Means | Coach Action |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Curiosity** | Loves trying new foods, workouts, routines | Prefers familiar, proven approaches | High: rotate plans. Low: keep plans stable. |

## **4.4 Goals, Motivators & Barriers**

These are derived from the contextual questionnaire. Present them as concise, labeled items \- not paragraphs.

| Field | Content Rule |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Primary Goal** | State the goal in the person's own words if available, otherwise use the category (e.g., muscle gain, fat loss, energy, stress reduction). Always connect subsequent guidance to this goal. |
| **What Motivates Them** | Derived from persona \+ contextual data. State what actually drives them to act \- not aspirational motivation. Example: 'Visible proof of progress' not 'achieving their dreams.' |
| **What Drains Them** | Specific environments, pressures, or expectations that reduce adherence. These are coaching red lines \- things to actively avoid in plan design. |
| **Top Barrier** | The single biggest obstacle from contextual data (e.g., lack of time, inconsistency, emotional eating). Every plan element should account for this barrier. |

## **4.5 Blind Spots (Per Persona)**

Each persona has a characteristic blind spot \- a pattern they cannot easily see from the inside. This is one of the highest-value sections of the guide because it gives the coach insight the client does not have about themselves.

| Persona | Blind Spot Pattern | Coach Response |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Steady Elephant** | Confuses routine compliance with actual progress. May follow the plan perfectly but not notice it stopped working weeks ago. | Periodically challenge the plan itself, not just execution. Ask: 'Is this still moving you forward?' |
| **Pack Wolf** | Mistakes social energy for personal commitment. Feels motivated in the group but has no system for when they are alone. | Build a solo fallback into every group plan. Test: can they do one session alone this week? |
| **Watchful Deer** | One disruption triggers an extended pause. The cost is not the missed session but the days spent waiting to feel ready again. | Pre-write a restart protocol. Make the restart action specific and effortless. |
| **Curious Fox** | Confuses exploring new approaches with making progress. Constantly starts but rarely builds depth in any one system. | Set a minimum commitment window (e.g., 4 weeks) before any plan change is allowed. |
| **Steadfast Bear** | Pushes through warning signals from their body. Reads fatigue as weakness rather than a recovery need. | Build mandatory recovery rules into the plan. Frame rest as performance strategy, not weakness. |
| **Shielded Turtle** | Interprets any discomfort as a sign the plan is wrong. Abandons setups before giving them time to work. | Set expectations upfront: discomfort in week 1-2 is normal. Distinguish bad fit from adjustment period. |

## **4.6 Adherence Risk Signals (Per Persona)**

These are behavioral signals the coach should monitor. They predict dropout or breakdown before it becomes visible in metrics.

| Persona | Warning Signals | What It Actually Means |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Steady Elephant** | Rigid adherence even when results plateau Resistance to any plan modification Anxiety when routine is disrupted | Over-attachment to the system. Needs permission to adapt without feeling like a failure. |
| **Pack Wolf** | Skips sessions when workout partner is unavailable Drops tracking when not being watched Energy crashes after group accountability ends | Has not built internal motivation. The group was carrying them. Needs solo habits. |
| **Watchful Deer** | Extended silence after a missed session Sudden over-compliance or intensification Requests to start over or redo the plan | Shame-withdrawal cycle. The silence is not compliance \- it is avoidance. Check in privately. |
| **Curious Fox** | Requests to change the plan every 1-2 weeks Researching alternative approaches mid-plan Excitement about a new method before finishing current one | Novelty-seeking, not plan failure. Hold the commitment window. Redirect curiosity to within-plan variation. |
| **Steadfast Bear** | Training through pain or illness Dismissing recovery recommendations Increasing volume or intensity without coach input | Competitive override. They are pushing past their body's signals. Enforce recovery as non-negotiable. |
| **Shielded Turtle** | Stops tracking without explanation Avoids check-in conversations Plans to 'start fresh next month' | Pre-withdrawal. They have already mentally quit. Simplify the plan immediately \- do not add. |

## **5.3 The 3-Layer Validation Check**

Apply this to every element of the plan before presenting it. The framework is universal but the criteria are persona-specific.

**Layer 1: Psychologically Appropriate?**

Does this plan element match the person's operating style? Would it feel safe, motivating, and sustainable for someone with this personality?

| Persona | Psychologically Appropriate Means... |
| :---- | :---- |
| Steady Elephant | Predictable, systematic, routine-compatible. No surprises. |
| Pack Wolf | Social, shared, community-connected. Not isolating. |
| Watchful Deer | Private, low-pressure, clearly defined. No exposure or shame risk. |
| Curious Fox | Novel, explorative, learning-oriented. Not repetitive or rigid. |
| Steadfast Bear | Challenging, measurable, performance-driven. Not easy or vague. |
| Shielded Turtle | Minimal, effortless, zero-pressure. Not ambitious or complex. |

**Layer 2: Behaviorally Realistic?**

Would a real person with this barrier profile actually do this consistently? Is the friction low enough given their life constraints?

| Common Barrier | Realism Check |
| :---- | :---- |
| Lack of time | Can this be done in under 20 minutes? Does it require preparation? |
| Inconsistency | Does this work even if they do it 2 out of 5 days? Is partial credit built in? |
| Emotional eating | Does this approach avoid guilt framing? Is there a non-judgmental backup? |
| Perfectionism | Is 'good enough' defined? Can they succeed without doing it perfectly? |
| Low activation energy | Can they start this without any setup? Is the first step trivially easy? |
| Overwhelm | Is this one thing? Or does it require remembering multiple steps? |

**Layer 3: Environmentally Executable?**

Can they actually do this in their real life? Do they have the space, tools, support, and access?

| Check | Question to Ask |
| :---- | :---- |
| Physical space | Do they have a gym, home space, or access to the recommended environment? |
| Time windows | Are the recommended times actually free in their schedule? |
| Social context | Does their household, partner, or work environment support or conflict with this? |
| Tools and tracking | Do they have/want the tracking tool being recommended? Is it too complex? |
| Food access | Can they realistically access the recommended foods given their location and budget? |

## **5.4 Monitoring & Pivot Triggers**

These are shown as a two-column table on Page 3\. Signals to Watch (left) and Pivot Triggers (right). Content is persona-specific \- use the risk signals from Section 4.6 to populate Signals to Watch, and the coach responses from Section 4.5 to inform Pivot Triggers.

**Universal pivot rule: If 2+ plan elements are consistently missed for more than 1 week, the plan is too complex. Simplify before adding anything.**

## **5.5 Review Cadence**

This is universal across personas with minor persona-specific notes:

| Period | Coach Action |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Weeks 1-2** | Verify the plan is understood and the recovery protocol is written. Check environment fit. Keep communication low-pressure. For cautious personas (Deer, Turtle): let them settle in. For social personas (Wolf): ensure solo backup exists. For performance personas (Bear): set recovery expectations early. |
| **Weeks 3-4** | Assess whether the structure is holding. If adherence is strong, reinforce \- do not add complexity. If dropping off, simplify. For explorative personas (Fox): redirect novelty-seeking within the plan. For routine personas (Elephant): check if they are plateauing without noticing. |
| **Week 6+** | Full plan review. Assess whether the setup still fits or if the person has shifted. Adjust, never overhaul. Consider whether a trait deviation has emerged that changes the coaching approach. |
